# Purpose

Pentimento is not a movie application.

It is a design research project investigating what a person needs when software narrates their life from their archive: evidence for every claim, honest uncertainty, and — above all — a right of reply.

Algorithmic autobiography is already mass-produced. Every year-in-review, every auto-curated memory reel, every "your top genre was..." asserts a story about a person, cites nothing, doubts nothing, and accepts no correction.

Pentimento builds the missing half of that conversation.

Movie archives are the first medium through which this question is explored.

The final product is evidence of the research—not the research itself.

---

# The Research Question

> **When software narrates a person's life from their archive, what does the person need in order to contest, correct, and co-own that story?**

The originating question — how interactive systems might help people recognize meaningful changes in their cultural identity through the media they consume — remains the project's first domain instance. The reframed question is what it was always underneath.

If a feature does not help answer this question, it does not belong in the project.

---

# The Observation

Current platforms remember consumption.

* Movies watched
* Ratings
* Reviews
* Watchlists
* Likes
* Statistics

They preserve events.

They rarely preserve transformation.

People often remember that they changed, but struggle to explain how that change happened.

Pentimento investigates whether software can make that evolution visible.

---

# Why Movies?

Movies are not the subject of the research.

They are the research medium.

Movies are uniquely suited because they are:

* Intentionally chosen.
* Emotionally engaging.
* Publicly discussed.
* Frequently revisited.
* Already documented through platforms like Letterboxd.

They provide one of the richest long-term records of cultural behavior available today.

The conceptual framework should eventually be applicable to books, music, fashion, games, design, photography, architecture, and other cultural domains.

---

# The Core Thesis

People do not experience culture as isolated pieces of media.

They experience periods of curiosity.

These periods gradually influence long-term preferences, which eventually become part of identity.

Current digital products record isolated events.

Pentimento attempts to reveal the evolution occurring between those events.

---

# What Pentimento Is

Pentimento is:

* a design research investigation
* a sensemaking interface
* a reflection tool
* a collaborative interpretation system
* an exploration of cultural identity
* an experiment in explainable human-AI interaction

---

# What Pentimento Is Not

Pentimento is not:

* a Letterboxd redesign
* a recommendation engine
* an AI movie assistant
* a social network
* a review platform
* a statistics dashboard
* a movie database
* an entertainment app

If the project begins resembling any of these, it has drifted from its purpose.

---

# The Contribution

The project aims to contribute an interaction grammar for contestable machine narratives:

**propose → show evidence → accept, reframe, or strike → correction outranks reading → the revision carries the scar.**

Rather than organizing media around content, ratings, or recommendations, it organizes an archive around human change — and then gives the human final authority over what that change meant.

Instead of asking:

> "What did you watch?"

Pentimento asks:

> "What changed — and who gets to say?"

---

# The Design Principles

## 1. Reflection over Consumption

Every interaction should help people understand something about themselves rather than simply consume more content.

---

## 2. Interpretation over Prediction

The system should prioritize helping people make sense of their past rather than predicting their future.

---

## 3. Evidence over Assertion

The system should never make unsupported claims.

Every interpretation must be explainable through observable evidence.

The product proposes hypotheses.

Users decide whether those hypotheses feel true.

---

## 4. Collaboration over Automation

The system and the user construct meaning together.

The system is never the authority.

---

## 5. Curiosity over Optimization

The project is not trying to maximize engagement.

It is trying to encourage meaningful exploration.

---

## 6. Questions over Answers

The best outcome is not certainty.

It is better questions.

The product should make people curious about themselves.

---

# The User Experience

The product should feel like:

* investigating a personal archive
* rediscovering forgotten memories
* reading an autobiography written collaboratively
* talking to an insightful friend
* connecting pieces of your own history

The product should never feel like:

* filling out surveys
* using analytics software
* being judged by AI
* taking a personality test
* browsing another streaming service

---

# Success Criteria

A successful reader should leave having felt one or both of these:

**Recognition** —

> "I never noticed that."

> "That's exactly what happened."

> "That explains why my taste changed."

**Standing** —

> "That's not what happened — and it listened."

> "My words outranked its reading, and the page proves it."

The second kind matters more. Any retrospective can produce recognition; only Pentimento is built to honor refusal. The project succeeds when people feel read *and* feel entitled to talk back — not when they are impressed by technology.

---

# Portfolio Goals

This project should prove that I can:

* identify an overlooked asymmetry between people and software
* take a public position on it — software that narrates a person owes that person a right of reply
* ground the position in literature, checked rather than recalled
* construct an original interaction grammar around it
* build the working proof and the research instrument to test it

It should clearly demonstrate a different capability from Invisible Interfaces and Interaction Atlas. (This list is kept in sync with PORTFOLIO.md's "What This Project Must Prove.")

---

# The Three Portfolio Questions

Every design decision must answer "yes."

## Question 1

Does this prove something different from my previous projects?

If not, remove it.

---

## Question 2

Does this strengthen the research contribution?

If not, simplify it.

---

## Question 3

Would a real person naturally understand and care about this?

If not, redesign it.

---

# The Fourth Question

Can the product explain itself?

Every interpretation should be supported by observable evidence.

Nothing should feel magical.

Nothing should feel arbitrary.

Trust is more important than intelligence.

---

# Non-Negotiables

* Research drives design.
* Theory drives implementation.
* The interaction model is more important than the interface.
* Simplicity is preferred over feature count.
* Beautiful visuals cannot compensate for weak ideas.
* Every feature must reinforce the central thesis.
* Human understanding is more important than AI capability.
* The project should remain understandable without mentioning AI.

---

# Failure Conditions

Pentimento has failed if people describe it as:

* "A better Letterboxd."
* "Movie recommendations with AI."
* "A movie tracking app."
* "A visualization project."
* "A portfolio concept."

Pentimento succeeds when people instead describe it as:

> "A new way of understanding how cultural experiences shape us."

---

# North Star

The project is complete when someone finishes using it and quietly says:

> "I never realized my journey looked like that."
